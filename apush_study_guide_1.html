⚡ Top 5 AP Exam Strategies

  • MCQ: Always identify the stimulus's time period first — 90% of questions are answerable from context + knowledge combined.
  • SAQ: Use A-B-C structure: Answer the prompt directly, Back it up with specific evidence, Connect to broader context.
  • DBQ: Categorize docs before writing. Aim to use all 7. Find the complexity angle (corroboration or tension between docs).
  • LEQ: Write a defensible thesis in the first paragraph — don't warm up slowly. Make a claim, not just a restatement.
  • Time: Never spend more than 65 seconds per MCQ. Skip and return. The SAQ/LEQ/DBQ is where points multiply.

SAQ / LEQ / DBQ Guide

Rubrics, strategies, sentence starters, and model responses

SAQ — Short Answer Question

  • 3 parts (a, b, c) — each worth 1 point = 3 points total
  • No thesis required — just direct, specific answers
  • Aim for 3–6 sentences per part
  • Always name specific people, events, acts, or dates
  • Use the A-B-C formula: Answer → Back up → Connect

Sample SAQ Prompt

"Use the following image and your knowledge of United States history to answer (a), (b), and (c). Briefly explain ONE cause of sectional tensions in the 1850s. Briefly explain ONE effect of the Kansas-Nebraska Act. Briefly evaluate ONE way the Republican Party emerged from this crisis."

Model Response (Part a)

Answer: One cause of sectional tensions in the 1850s was the debate over whether slavery would expand into territories acquired from Mexico after the Mexican-American War. Evidence: The Compromise of 1850, including the controversial Fugitive Slave Act, temporarily resolved this dispute but deepened Northern resentment by requiring Northerners to assist in the capture of escaped enslaved people. Connection: This forced Northern complicity in slavery's enforcement radicalized moderates and made compromise increasingly impossible by the mid-1850s.

🚫 SAQ Common Mistakes

  • Writing a vague answer without a specific name or event
  • Answering a different part than what was asked
  • Writing an intro paragraph — just answer directly
  • Repeating the same evidence across multiple parts

LEQ — Long Essay Question

  • 6 points possible — 40 minutes suggested
  • Choose 1 of 3 prompts (different time periods)
  • Must have: thesis, contextualization, evidence, complexity
  • Think of it as: intro + 2–3 body paragraphs + conclusion (optional)

💡 Thesis Formula for LEQ

A strong LEQ thesis = Claim + Categories of Analysis


Example prompt: "Evaluate the extent to which the Civil War transformed American society from 1865 to 1877."


Strong thesis: "Although the Civil War brought significant legal transformations through the Reconstruction Amendments, its impact on American society was ultimately limited by persistent racial violence, the failure of land redistribution, and the political compromise of 1877, which restored white supremacist control throughout the South."

Notice: Makes a clear claim (limited transformation), gives 3 categories (violence, land, politics), and sets up a line of reasoning for the entire essay.

📄 HAPP Sourcing — How to Write It

  • Historical Situation: "Written during the height of industrialization, [author]'s perspective reflects the anxieties of urban workers facing displacement..."
  • Audience: "Because this speech was directed at a Southern audience, [author] likely downplayed the brutality of slavery to maintain credibility..."
  • Purpose: "As a piece of abolitionist propaganda intended to build Northern opposition, this document likely overstates..."
  • Point of View: "As a formerly enslaved person, [author] had direct personal experience that makes her account more credible than that of a planter..."

🗂 Doc Categorization Strategy (15-minute reading period)

  • Read each source line before the document itself
  • Sort docs into 2–3 thematic groups (Pro/Con, Economic/Social/Political, etc.)
  • Star 3 docs you'll use for HAPP sourcing
  • Identify the "outlier" doc that complicates your argument — use it for complexity
  • Sketch a 3-sentence thesis before writing a word of the essay

What Makes a Thesis "Defensible"?

  • It makes a specific historical argument — not just a restatement of the prompt
  • It can be disagreed with — a good thesis is debatable, not a fact
  • It sets up categories of analysis that your body paragraphs will prove
  • It appears in the intro or conclusion (not buried in the middle)

Sentence Starters for Thesis

  • "Although [concession], [your argument] because [categories]..."
  • "While [one factor] contributed to [event], [bigger factor] was ultimately more significant due to [reasons]..."
  • "[Event] fundamentally transformed [subject] by [category 1], [category 2], and [category 3], even as [continuity] persisted..."

Contextualization — The Point Most Students Miss

  • Must be a broader historical development that predates or surrounds the prompt's topic
  • Must be described in detail (2–4 sentences minimum)
  • Must be explicitly connected to the argument — don't just describe it and move on
  • It is NOT just mentioning a related event — it must set the stage for your argument

Examples by Era

Prompt about Civil War / Sectional Conflict
"In the decades following the Missouri Compromise of 1820, the question of slavery's expansion into new territories became the defining fault line in American politics. The Market Revolution of the 1820s–1840s deepened economic divergence between the industrializing North and the cotton-dependent South, creating two societies with fundamentally incompatible labor systems and political interests. This structural tension meant that any territorial acquisition — such as those gained in the Mexican-American War — would reignite the slavery question with intensifying ferocity, ultimately making compromise impossible by the 1850s."
Prompt about Progressive Era
"The rapid industrialization of the Gilded Age (1870s–1890s) transformed the United States into the world's largest industrial economy, but at enormous social cost. The concentration of wealth in the hands of industrial titans like Carnegie and Rockefeller, combined with brutal working conditions, widespread urban poverty documented by muckrakers like Jacob Riis, and the political corruption of the spoils system, created widespread public demand for reform. This Gilded Age context of inequality and corporate power directly fueled the Progressive movement's push for government intervention."
