When the Wolves
Came Back
Modeling predator-prey dynamics on Isle Royale
System: Predator-Prey Ecology (Canis lupus / Alces alces)
Dataset: Isle Royale Wolf-Moose Project, 1959–2026
Course: AP Biology  ·  Submitted: May 20, 2026
Introduction

Driving Question

How can a model represent, explain, and — when the real world doesn't cooperate — help us revise our understanding of a biological system?

Isle Royale is a 210-square-mile island sitting in Lake Superior, about 15 miles off the Minnesota shore. It is remote enough to be almost perfectly isolated, which makes it one of the most valuable natural laboratories on Earth. Moose arrived in the early 1900s. For decades they had no predators, and their population swung between boom and crash driven entirely by starvation and disease. Then, in 1948, a pack of gray wolves crossed an ice bridge from Canada — and suddenly there was a predator. Scientists arrived ten years later to watch what happened. They are still watching. The Isle Royale Wolf-Moose Project is the longest continuously running predator-prey study in the world, now in its 68th year.

What they found over those 68 years is the subject of this report. The simple model predicts oscillation. Reality delivers disease outbreaks, genetic collapse, climate shifts, and a government wolf-translocation program. The model is still useful — but only after it's been broken and rebuilt.

Section 1

Model Diagram

The initial model is based on the Lotka-Volterra equations, developed independently by Alfred Lotka (1925) and Vito Volterra (1926). The model assumes two species in a closed system: a prey population that grows exponentially when the predator is absent, and a predator population that depends entirely on prey for survival. Key terms

Lotka-Volterra model

Mathematical framework describing oscillating predator and prey populations in a closed system.

Carrying capacity (K)

Maximum population size an environment can sustainably support given available resources.

Functional response

The relationship between predator kill rate and prey density (Types I, II, III).

Numerical response

Change in predator population size in response to change in prey population density.

Negative feedback loop

A regulatory mechanism where a change in a variable triggers a response that opposes the initial change.

Trophic cascade

Indirect effects of a predator on lower trophic levels, including vegetation (e.g., wolf → moose → balsam fir).

Section 2

System Explanation

The predator-prey system on Isle Royale operates through two interlocking feedback loops. In the first, moose populations grow exponentially when wolf populations are low — more food, less predation pressure, higher calf survival. As moose abundance increases, wolves have more prey to hunt. They eat more, reproduce more, and their population rises. In the second loop, as wolf populations climb, predation intensity increases. Fewer moose survive to reproductive age. The moose population contracts. With less prey available, wolves begin to starve and their numbers fall. Moose recover. The cycle repeats.

"Kill rate should be greatest during years when the ratio of moose per wolf is greatest — abundant prey makes them easier to find, and fewer wolves means less time lost to territorial conflict." — Isle Royale Wolf-Moose Project, population dynamics analysis

This is the numerical response illustrated in the image your teacher provided: more prey → more predators, fewer prey → fewer predators. But predation is not simply a function of prey density. It also depends on predator behavior — specifically, the functional response.

A Type II functional response (the curve seen in most real predators, including wolves) shows that as prey density increases, kill rate rises quickly at first, then levels off. This is because wolves spend time hunting, handling prey, and consuming it. There is a ceiling on how many moose a pack can kill per unit time, regardless of how many moose are available. A Type III response adds a low-density refuge effect: when moose are very scarce, wolves switch to easier prey (beavers, in this case), and moose predation rate drops. This gives moose a slight protective buffer at very low densities — which may help explain why the Isle Royale moose population has never quite reached zero, even in its worst years.

Ecosystem connection

Wolves regulate moose not just through direct killing but through the ecology of fear — moose in high-predation areas reduce their time browsing in open areas and move more frequently, which distributes grazing pressure across the island. When wolves disappeared in the early 2000s, moose browsed balsam fir seedlings so intensively that the trees virtually stopped growing. In the 2026 survey, researchers reported that balsam fir saplings were averaging about one inch of growth per year, versus an expected eight inches in low-browsing conditions — a measurable, vegetation-level effect of predation pressure.

Model components and interactions

The system has four primary components: (1) vegetation (balsam fir, willows, and other browse species) as the energy input; (2) moose as primary consumer and prey; (3) wolves as apex predator; and (4) the physical environment (winter severity, ice bridges, island isolation) as a boundary condition that modifies all three.

The key interaction is predation: wolves consume moose at a rate determined by both moose density and pack size. This predation simultaneously removes moose from the prey population and — via the nutrition gained — supports wolf reproduction. When moose are abundant relative to wolves, this interaction strongly favors wolves. When wolves are abundant relative to moose, predation pressure becomes unsustainable: packs compete with each other, prey per wolf falls, and wolf mortality from starvation increases.

A secondary but critical interaction is grazing: moose consume vegetation at a rate that scales with moose density. At very high moose densities, browse resources become depleted, independently limiting moose population growth even without wolf predation. This adds a logistic growth constraint to the prey equation — the system is not purely Lotka-Volterra, because prey are not unlimited even in the absence of predators.

Section 3

Data Analysis

The following graphs use data from the Isle Royale Wolf-Moose Project (Michigan Technological University), which has conducted aerial surveys every winter since 1959. The dataset represents 68 years of continuous field observation — one of the richest predator-prey records in ecological science.

Graph 1 — Wolf and Moose Populations on Isle Royale, 1959–2026
Annual counts from Michigan Technological University aerial surveys. Note inverse relationship between populations and key events affecting system dynamics.
Wolf population (left axis × 40)
Moose population (right axis)
Population data for Isle Royale wolves and moose, 1959-2026.
Data interpretation — Graph 1

The data shows a clear inverse relationship between wolf and moose populations across most of the study period. When wolf numbers peaked in the late 1970s (~50 wolves), moose populations were at relatively low levels. Following the 1980 wolf population crash (caused by canine parvovirus introduced by a visitor's domestic dog), moose rebounded strongly, eventually reaching a peak near 2,400 individuals in the mid-1990s. A severe winter in 1996 and a tick outbreak simultaneously crashed the moose population. The most dramatic recent trend is the post-2018 wolf translocation: the National Park Service introduced 19 wolves from external populations, and by 2026 wolf numbers had climbed to 37 — while moose fell from ~2,000 to ~524, a 75% decline. This trend supports the model's prediction that increased predator density drives prey population decline.

Graph 2 — Predation Rate vs. Prey Density: Functional Response Curves
Theoretical functional response types applied to Isle Royale data. Type II best describes wolf predation behavior; Type III may apply at very low moose densities (<500 individuals).
Type I (linear, no saturation)
Type II (hyperbolic saturation — wolves)
Type III (sigmoidal, low-density refuge)
Functional response curves Types I, II, and III showing prey consumption rate vs prey population density.
Data interpretation — Graph 2

This trend shows that wolf predation on moose does not increase linearly with moose abundance. Instead, it saturates — wolves can only process a certain number of moose per unit time regardless of how many are available. This is characteristic of a Type II functional response, which is observed in large mammalian predators generally. At the low end of the moose density range (below ~500 animals, where Isle Royale sits in 2026), there is evidence of a shift toward Type III behavior: wolves increasingly supplement their diet with beavers when moose are scarce, effectively reducing predation pressure on moose at their lowest densities. This supports the model because it explains why the moose population has not collapsed to zero even with 37 wolves — the functional response provides a partial refuge.

Graph 3 — Post-Translocation Population Dynamics, 2018–2026
Following reintroduction of 19 wolves in 2018–2019, moose population declined 75% while wolf population tripled. Data from Michigan Tech annual winter surveys.
Wolf count
Moose count (÷ 50 for scale)
Wolf and moose counts from 2018 to 2026, post-translocation period.
Data interpretation — Graph 3

The data from 2018–2026 constitutes a near-experimental test of the predator-prey model. When predator density was externally increased (wolves translocated), prey density fell — rapidly and substantially. This supports the model because it confirms the directional prediction: predator increase → prey decrease. The rate of decline (75% moose reduction over 7 years) is also ecologically consistent with the estimated 24% annual predation mortality rate recorded in the 2026 winter survey. Notably, researchers recorded zero moose calves in the 2026 aerial count — a first in 68 years of surveys — suggesting that predation pressure is now suppressing recruitment as well as adult survival, which would accelerate future population decline beyond what the basic Lotka-Volterra model predicts.

Section 4

Primary Source Integration

Two primary research articles were selected to ground this model in published science. Both are peer-reviewed and represent primary research (original data collection and analysis, not review articles).

Source 1: "Modeling Moose–Wolf interactions in Isle Royale National Park using sparse identification of nonlinear dynamics"
Mondal et al. (2025) — Scientific Reports (Nature Publishing Group) · DOI: 10.1038/s41598-025-08874-7

Research question: Can mathematical models be derived directly from 60 years of field data, without pre-assuming the model structure?


Methods: Researchers applied the SINDy algorithm (Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics) to annual wolf and moose population counts from 1959–2019. Rather than fitting parameters to a pre-chosen model (like Lotka-Volterra), they allowed the mathematical structure to emerge from the data itself.


Key findings: The wolf population ranged from 2 to 50 individuals (mean: 21), and the moose population ranged from 385 to 2,398 (mean: 1,021). The data-derived model revealed oscillatory dynamics consistent with Lotka-Volterra predictions, but also identified periods of "fragility" — years when the system was particularly sensitive to external shocks. These fragile periods correspond closely to known crisis events (the 1980 disease outbreak, the 1996 severe winter).


Connection to model: This study validates the fundamental structure of our initial Lotka-Volterra model, confirming that predator-prey oscillation is a genuine feature of the Isle Royale system — not an artifact of model assumptions. It also motivates our model revision: the identification of "fragile" periods implies that external perturbations (disease, climate, human intervention) can push the system far from the simple equilibrium the basic model predicts.

Source 2: "Ecological Studies of Wolves on Isle Royale — Annual Report 2025–2026"
Hoy, S. & Peterson, R. (2026) — Michigan Technological University, College of Forest Resources and Environmental Science

Research question: What are current population sizes and predation rates, and what is the trajectory of the wolf-moose system following the 2018–2019 wolf translocation?


Methods: Aerial surveys conducted January 22 – March 3, 2026, in fixed-wing aircraft over the island. Wolf packs were identified individually; moose were counted by transect survey. Predation rate was estimated from kill sites located during surveys and from GPS-collar data on individual wolves.


Key findings: 37 wolves organized in three packs (West, East, Northeast; 10–13 wolves each) and approximately 524 moose. Wolf predation accounted for an estimated 24% of the moose population in the preceding year — near the highest predation rate ever recorded in the study. No moose calves were observed, suggesting recruitment failure in addition to adult mortality. Balsam fir growth remains suppressed (~1 inch/year vs. ~8 inches expected) despite declining moose density.


Connection to model: This study provides the most current empirical data for our model. The 24% annual predation mortality rate is a direct measurement of the parameter β (predation rate) in the Lotka-Volterra equations. The observation of zero calves adds a recruitment dimension that the basic two-variable model does not capture — motivating the revised model's inclusion of age structure and reproductive suppression effects.

Model Evaluation and Revision

Limitations of the initial model

Revised model

Based on the evidence above, the revised model incorporates four major changes to the initial Lotka-Volterra structure:

Figure 2. Revised predator-prey model incorporating age structure, external perturbations, and the ecology of fear pathway. Predation arrow routes above the boxes; prey-growth arrow routes below. The ecology-of-fear pathway exits the right side of the wolf box and loops back to vegetation without crossing other elements.

The revised model makes four substantive changes. First, both populations are now age-structured: moose are tracked as calves and adults separately, because predation strongly targets calves and the 2026 zero-calf observation cannot be represented in a single-variable moose population. Second, a genetic diversity variable is added to the wolf population — the near-extinction of the wolf population due to inbreeding (down to 2 individuals by 2016) was a major system perturbation invisible to the basic model. Third, external perturbation pathways are explicitly added, representing disease (canine parvovirus), climate (severe winters, tick outbreaks), and immigration/management (wolf translocation). These are modeled as stochastic inputs that can shift populations outside the normal oscillation range. Fourth, the ecology of fear is added as an indirect pathway: wolf presence suppresses moose movement in ways that reduce vegetative browse pressure — a feedback that connects the predator back to the vegetation layer without going through direct predation.

These revisions move the model from a simple two-variable oscillator toward a more realistic multi-variable system. The tradeoff is complexity: the revised model is harder to solve analytically and requires numerical simulation or ongoing field data to parameterize. But it makes better predictions — particularly for periods when external shocks push the system far from the Lotka-Volterra equilibrium, which on Isle Royale is most of the time.
