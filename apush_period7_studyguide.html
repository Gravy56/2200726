Ch. 18

The Progressive Era

1900 – 1916 ▼

"New immigration" — what group, what period, and why was their quest for freedom a struggle?

Why did socialism thrive during the Progressive Era, and who led it?

What was "new feminism" and how did it spearhead broader reforms?

In what ways did Progressivism include both democratic and anti-democratic impulses?

How did each Progressive Era president view the federal government's role?

1900–1911
Triangle Shirtwaist Fire
A 1911 factory fire in NYC that killed 146 garment workers, mostly immigrant women, due to locked exits and poor safety. Galvanized the labor movement and led to major workplace safety legislation and fire codes in New York and across the nation.
tap to expand
1900s
Muckrakers
Investigative journalists who exposed corruption, unsafe conditions, and corporate abuses. Figures like Upton Sinclair (The Jungle), Ida Tarbell (Standard Oil), and Jacob Riis (How the Other Half Lives) helped shape public opinion and drive Progressive legislation.
tap to expand
1900s
Child Labor
The widespread employment of children in hazardous industries — mines, factories, mills — was a Progressive Era target. Reformers like Lewis Hine documented abuse; the Keating-Owen Act (1916) was the first federal attempt to limit it (later struck down). Eventually restricted by New Deal laws.
tap to expand
1890s–1910s
Ellis Island & Angel Island
Ellis Island (NY) processed millions of European immigrants; Angel Island (CA) processed and often detained Asian immigrants under far harsher conditions. Symbolize both the promise and the inequalities of American immigration policy in the era of "new immigration."
tap to expand
1900s
AFL vs. IWW
The American Federation of Labor (craft union, skilled workers, Samuel Gompers) vs. the Industrial Workers of the World ("Wobblies" — radical, unskilled workers, all industries). Represented a fundamental split in labor strategy: incremental reform vs. revolutionary industrial unionism.
tap to expand
1900s–1910s
Birth-Control Movement
Led by Margaret Sanger, who challenged laws criminalizing contraception. Argued reproductive freedom was essential to women's autonomy. Founded what became Planned Parenthood. Represented the intersection of feminism, public health, and class politics.
tap to expand
1902
Coal Miner's Strike of 1902
United Mine Workers struck in PA; TR intervened and forced arbitration (first time federal gov't sided with labor, not capital). Established the precedent of presidential mediation in labor disputes and signaled a new activist role for the executive branch.
tap to expand
1906
Pure Food and Drug Act
Passed in 1906 following public outrage (partly spurred by Upton Sinclair's The Jungle). Prohibited adulterated or mislabeled food and drugs sold in interstate commerce. Created the foundation for the modern FDA. Key example of federal consumer protection.
tap to expand
1901–1909
Roosevelt and Conservation
TR set aside 150 million acres of national forests, created 5 national parks, 18 national monuments, and 51 wildlife refuges. Signed the Newlands Reclamation Act (1902). Distinguished between preservation (Muir) and sustainable use (Pinchot); placed conservation on the national agenda.

Safe for Democracy — World War I

1916 – 1920 ▼

How did Progressive Era presidents promote American power overseas?

What two main events drew the U.S. into WWI?

How were civil liberties infringed upon during WWI?

How did the National Woman's Party change suffrage advocacy?

How were African Americans excluded from Progressive reforms? Compare DuBois vs. Garvey.

Why was 1919 a watershed year?

How did Wilson's "self-determination" animate third-world intellectuals?

1903
Panama Canal Zone
The U.S. backed Panamanian independence from Colombia in 1903 to secure canal rights. The Canal (completed 1914) connected Atlantic and Pacific, reducing shipping time dramatically. Symbolized American imperial reach and strategic economic interest in hemispheric dominance.
tap to expand
1904
Roosevelt's Corollary
Extension of the Monroe Doctrine (1904): the U.S. claimed the right to intervene in Latin American nations with "chronic wrongdoing" to prevent European interference. Justified repeated interventions (Nicaragua, Haiti, Dominican Republic). Seen across Latin America as Yankee imperialism.
tap to expand
1909–1913
Dollar Diplomacy
Taft's policy of using U.S. investment and financial control to advance foreign policy goals, especially in Latin America and China. Backed by force when necessary. Replaced gunboat diplomacy with economic leverage but still served U.S. imperial interests.
tap to expand
1917
Zimmermann Telegram
Secret German telegram (intercepted by Britain) proposing a military alliance with Mexico against the U.S., offering Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. Outraged American public; major catalyst for entering WWI in April 1917.
tap to expand
1917
Committee on Public Information (CPI)
George Creel's propaganda bureau created to manufacture consent for WWI. Used posters, films, pamphlets, and "Four-Minute Men" speakers. Stoked nationalist fervor and anti-German hysteria; set a precedent for modern government propaganda and the use of mass media in wartime.
tap to expand
1919
18th Amendment
Prohibited the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors (Prohibition). Ratified 1919, took effect 1920. Driven by Progressive temperance movement. Led to rise of organized crime, speakeasies, and widespread flouting of the law; repealed by 21st Amendment (1933).
tap to expand
1917
Espionage Act of 1917
Made it illegal to obstruct military recruitment or interfere with war operations. Used to prosecute antiwar voices including Eugene Debs (sentenced to 10 years). The Supreme Court upheld it in Schenck v. U.S. (1919) with Holmes's "clear and present danger" test. Major civil liberties violation.
tap to expand
1918
Sedition Act
Extended the Espionage Act to criminalize "disloyal, profane, scurrilous, or abusive language" about the U.S. government, flag, or military. Effectively suppressed all dissent. Targeted socialists, labor organizers, and immigrants. Repealed in 1920.
tap to expand
1900s
Color Line
W.E.B. DuBois's concept (from The Souls of Black Folk, 1903) describing racial segregation as the defining problem of the 20th century. The color line structured American life through Jim Crow, disenfranchisement, lynching, and exclusion from Progressive reform and WWI democracy.
tap to expand
1909
NAACP
National Association for the Advancement of Colored People — founded 1909 by DuBois, Ida B. Wells, and others. Fought for civil rights through legal challenges, publicity, and political lobbying. Key organization in the long civil rights struggle; won landmark cases including Brown v. Board (1954).
tap to expand
1920s
Garveyites
Followers of Marcus Garvey's UNIA (Universal Negro Improvement Association). Advocated Black nationalism, Pan-Africanism, and "Back to Africa." Mass movement among urban Black Americans in the 1920s; challenged the integrationist approach of the NAACP. Garvey deported in 1927.
tap to expand
1919–1920
Red Scare
Post-WWI wave of anti-communist fear; Palmer Raids (1919–20) arrested thousands of suspected radicals, many immigrants, with little due process. Accelerated nativist sentiment leading to the 1924 Immigration Act. ACLU founded in response. Foreshadowed 1950s McCarthyism.
tap to expand
1918
Fourteen Points
Wilson's 1918 peace plan: freedom of the seas, self-determination, open diplomacy, arms reduction, and a League of Nations. Inspired subject peoples worldwide but were largely ignored at Versailles. The Senate rejected U.S. membership in the League; Wilson's idealism crashed against political reality.
tap to expand
1919
League of Nations
Wilson's proposed international peacekeeping body (Article X of the Treaty of Versailles). Senate rejected it — isolationists and reservationists (led by Henry Cabot Lodge) refused to surrender U.S. sovereignty. The U.S. never joined. Weakened the League and set the stage for WWII.

Ch. 20

Business Culture to Great Depression — The Twenties

1920 – 1932 ▼

What products became staples of the 1920s and how did they create a "new society"?

How did Harding and Coolidge fuse government and business like no other time?

How did the Scopes Trial highlight the tension between fundamentalist and modernist?

How did the KKK's resurgence and Coolidge's race policy inspire the 1924 Immigration Act?

What were the causes of the Great Depression, and how effective were government responses by 1932?

1927
Sacco and Vanzetti
Italian immigrant anarchists convicted of murder in 1920 and executed in 1927 despite thin evidence. Case became international symbol of nativist hysteria, anti-radical bias, and failure of American justice. Widely seen as victims of the Red Scare and ethnic prejudice.
tap to expand
1920s
"Welfare Capitalism" vs. American Plan
Welfare capitalism: companies offered benefits (pensions, housing) to discourage unionization. American Plan: open-shop movement to eliminate unions entirely. Together they weakened organized labor in the 1920s prosperity decade. When the Depression hit, workers had no safety net.
tap to expand
1923
Equal Rights Amendment
First introduced in 1923 by Alice Paul and the NWP — proposed constitutional equality regardless of sex. Opposed by many progressives who feared it would overturn protective labor laws for women. Remained controversial throughout the period; not passed by Congress until 1972.
tap to expand
1920s
Flapper Girls
Symbol of 1920s "new woman" — bobbed hair, short skirts, jazz clubs, cigarettes. Challenged Victorian femininity and gender norms. Reflected new consumer culture, women's greater economic independence post-suffrage, and the cultural rebellion of the Jazz Age.
tap to expand
1923
Teapot Dome Scandal
Secretary of Interior Albert Fall secretly leased federal oil reserves at Teapot Dome, WY and Elk Hills, CA to private companies in exchange for bribes. First cabinet member imprisoned for crimes in office. Epitomized Harding administration corruption and government-business collusion.
tap to expand
1924–1928
McNary-Haugen Farm Bill
Proposed federal price supports for farm commodities — government would buy surpluses and sell abroad. Twice vetoed by Coolidge as government interference in the market. Highlighted the agricultural crisis of the 1920s and farmer political mobilization; presaged New Deal farm policy.
tap to expand
1930
Hays Code
Hollywood's self-censorship code (enforced from 1930s) that prohibited certain content in films — crime, sexuality, religious disrespect. Reflected cultural conservatism reacting against 1920s permissiveness. Showed the power of religious and civic groups to police popular culture.
tap to expand
1920
American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)
Founded 1920 by Roger Baldwin in response to the Palmer Raids and WWI civil liberties violations. Defended free speech, press, and due process. Represented John Scopes (1925); has fought landmark civil liberties cases ever since. Essential countervailing force to government repression.
tap to expand
1920s
"100 Percent Americanism"
Nativist ideology of the 1920s demanding cultural, linguistic, and racial conformity. Fueled KKK revival, the 1924 Immigration Act (quotas targeting Southern/Eastern Europeans and Asians), and anti-communist hysteria. Defined "real" Americans against immigrants, Catholics, Jews, and Blacks.
tap to expand
1920s
"Illegal Alien"
Term emerged after the 1924 Immigration Act made Mexican migration illegal for the first time (no quota assigned). Created a new category of criminalized worker; Border Patrol founded 1924. Began the pattern of mass deportations (including during the Depression) that defines modern immigration politics.
tap to expand
1920s
New Negro
Cultural movement (Harlem Renaissance) asserting Black dignity, artistic achievement, and political assertion. Alain Locke's 1925 anthology; Hughes, Hurston, Cullen. Harlem became center of Black cultural life. Represented rejection of accommodation and a new assertion of Black identity and pride.
tap to expand
1932
Bonus Marchers
WWI veterans who marched to Washington in 1932 demanding early payment of their promised bonus. Hoover ordered MacArthur to forcibly disperse them with tanks and tear gas. The violent response shocked the nation and destroyed Hoover's political standing; contributed to FDR's landslide election.
tap to expand
Ch. 21

The New Deal

1933 – 1939 ▼

Compare and contrast FDR and Theodore Roosevelt.

What were the major policy initiatives of the First Hundred Days? How did they transform the role of government?

What did the CIO set out to do, and what did they accomplish?

How did the Second New Deal differ from the First?

To what extent did New Deal benefits apply to women and minorities?

1933
Bank Holiday
FDR declared a 4-day national bank holiday on March 6, 1933 — his first major act. Stopped bank runs; Treasury inspected banks before reopening only the solvent ones. Restored public confidence. Accompanied by the Emergency Banking Act. The First Hundred Days began here.
tap to expand
1933
Glass-Steagall Act
Separated commercial and investment banking, preventing banks from using deposits for stock speculation. Created FDIC (federal deposit insurance). Addressed the reckless banking that contributed to the Depression. Partially repealed in 1999 — deregulation many blame for the 2008 financial crisis.
tap to expand
1933
National Recovery Administration (NRA)
Created codes for industry: fair wages, prices, hours, and collective bargaining. Struck down by SCOTUS in Schechter Poultry (1935) as unconstitutional delegation of congressional power. Symbolized First New Deal's attempt at cooperative economic planning and its constitutional limits.
tap to expand
1933
Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC)
Employed 3 million young men in national parks and forests — planting trees, building trails, fighting fires. Provided income sent home to families. Popular and successful; symbol of New Deal's conservation legacy. Excluded women; Black CCC workers often in segregated camps.
tap to expand
1933
Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA)
Federal corporation that built dams and hydroelectric plants across the Tennessee Valley — one of the poorest regions of the U.S. Provided cheap electricity, flood control, and economic development to 7 states. Most ambitious example of New Deal regional planning; still exists today.
tap to expand
1933
Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA)
Paid farmers to reduce crop production to raise prices. Controversial — destroyed food while people starved; Black sharecroppers often cut out of payments. Struck down in 1936 but reenacted. Established federal role in agricultural markets and transformed the political economy of farming.
tap to expand
1930s
Dust Bowl
Drought + over-farming of Great Plains led to catastrophic dust storms ("Black Blizzards") throughout the 1930s. Displaced 3.5 million people — "Okies" migrating to California. John Steinbeck's The Grapes of Wrath captured it. Led to federal soil conservation programs and migrant labor policies.
tap to expand
1933
21st Amendment
Repealed Prohibition (18th Amendment) in 1933. FDR supported repeal as an economic stimulus (taxable alcohol revenue) and as a morale boost. Symbolized the failure of government moralism and the pragmatic New Deal approach to Depression-era politics.
tap to expand
1930s
Huey Long
Louisiana governor and senator, "Kingfish." Populist critic of FDR from the left — "Share Our Wealth" plan proposed capping fortunes and guaranteeing minimum income. Assassinated 1935. His popularity forced FDR left toward the Second New Deal's more redistributive policies.
tap to expand
1935
Works Progress Administration (WPA)
Employed 8.5 million workers in public construction, arts, and literacy projects. Built airports, roads, schools; funded Federal Theatre and Writers Projects. Largest New Deal employment agency. Demonstrated government's capacity as employer of last resort. Transformed public infrastructure.
tap to expand
1935
Social Security Act
Created old-age insurance, unemployment insurance, and aid for the disabled and dependent children. Most consequential New Deal measure — established the American welfare state. Excluded farmworkers and domestic workers (disproportionately Black and Latino). Expanded significantly in subsequent decades.
tap to expand
1937
Court Packing Plan
FDR's attempt to add up to 6 new justices (one for each sitting justice over 70) after SCOTUS struck down New Deal programs. Congress rejected it — even Democrats opposed. Political blunder that damaged FDR's second term. But SCOTUS suddenly began upholding New Deal laws ("switch in time").
tap to expand
1930s
American Communist Party
CPUSA gained influence during the Depression as the only major organization actively fighting for Black civil rights (Scottsboro Boys), organizing unemployed workers, and building multiracial coalitions. Tied to Soviet Comintern policy; later devastated by Cold War repression. Critical to understanding 1930s labor and civil rights history.
tap to expand
1938
Minimum Wage Laws
Fair Labor Standards Act (1938) established federal minimum wage (25¢/hr), maximum 44-hour workweek, and abolished child labor in interstate commerce. Final major New Deal reform. Did not cover domestic workers or farmworkers (again excluding minorities). Established federal standards for labor.
tap to expand
1934
Indian New Deal
Indian Reorganization Act (1934), John Collier's "Indian New Deal" — ended Dawes Act allotments, restored tribal self-governance, and supported Native cultures. Most significant shift in federal Indian policy since the 1880s. Reversed forced assimilation; restored ~2 million acres. Contested by some Native leaders.
tap to expand
1935–1939
The Popular Front
Communist International strategy (1935) to unite liberals, socialists, and communists against fascism. In the U.S., it shaped labor organizing (CIO), cultural production, and civil rights activism. Created a broad left coalition in the 1930s. Collapsed with the Nazi-Soviet Pact (1939).
tap to expand
1931
Scottsboro Boys
Nine Black teenagers falsely accused of raping two white women in Alabama (1931). Convicted by all-white juries; SCOTUS overturned twice (Powell v. Alabama, Norris v. Alabama). ILD and CPUSA led the defense. Became international symbol of Jim Crow injustice and the struggle for Black civil rights.
tap to expand
1938
House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC)
Congressional committee created 1938, originally to investigate both Nazi and Communist influence. Became a platform for anti-communist witch hunts. Presaged McCarthyism; targeted Hollywood, labor unions, and civil rights activists. Chilling effect on free speech and political organizing.
tap to expand
Ch. 22

Fighting for the Four Freedoms — WWII

1939 – 1945 ▼

What steps led to American participation in WWII?

How did the U.S. mobilize resources and promote popular support for the war?

How did a war for "essential human freedoms" fail to protect the rights of Blacks, Native peoples, Japanese-Americans, and Mexican-Americans?

What role did African Americans play in WWII? How did the NAACP, CORE, and Black internationalists fight for equality postwar?

Was the U.S. justified in dropping atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki?

How did Bretton Woods positively position the U.S. in the postwar period?

1941
Four Freedoms
FDR's Jan. 1941 State of the Union articulated the four freedoms worth fighting for: freedom of speech, freedom of worship, freedom from want, freedom from fear. Defined American war aims ideologically; Norman Rockwell's paintings made them iconic. Created an ironic tension with domestic racism.
tap to expand
1933
Good Neighbor Policy
FDR's hemispheric policy — renounced military intervention in Latin America, repealed the Platt Amendment, withdrew from Haiti. Built goodwill and solidarity in WWII. Reversed TR's Corollary; acknowledged prior imperialism. Made Pan-American solidarity possible against the Axis.
tap to expand
1941
Lend-Lease Act
1941 act allowing FDR to lend or lease war materials to any nation vital to U.S. defense. Primarily aided Britain and later USSR. Got around neutrality laws. Committed U.S. resources to Allied cause before Pearl Harbor. "Arsenal of Democracy" — $50B total aid by war's end.
tap to expand
1942
Bataan "Death March"
After fall of Bataan (Philippines, April 1942), 76,000 Filipino and American POWs were force-marched 65 miles in brutal heat with no food or water; thousands died. Shocked American public; intensified Pacific war hatred and propaganda. Symbol of Japanese war crimes.
tap to expand
1944
D-Day (Operation Overlord)
June 6, 1944 Allied invasion of Normandy — 156,000 troops, 5,000 ships. Opened Western Front Stalin had demanded. Turned the tide in Western Europe; Germany now fighting on two fronts. Led to liberation of France and ultimate Allied victory in Europe (May 1945).
tap to expand
1942
Office of War Information
Federal agency managing wartime propaganda — films, posters, radio, print. Promoted unity, patriotism, and enemy hatred. Built on CPI model from WWI. Collaborated with Hollywood. Contradictions: promoted racial tolerance abroad while enforcing Jim Crow at home.
tap to expand
1942
War Advertising Council
Partnership between government and advertising industry to create pro-war consumer messaging. Promoted war bonds, rationing, and home front sacrifice. Created lasting infrastructure for public service advertising. Became the Ad Council after the war.
tap to expand
1942
Rosie the Riveter
Cultural icon of female industrial workers during WWII — 6 million women entered the workforce in war industries. J. Howard Miller's "We Can Do It!" poster. Symbolized transformation in gender roles; women proved competence in traditionally male jobs. Many fired after war to give jobs to veterans.
tap to expand
1944
Economic Bill of Rights
FDR's 1944 State of the Union proposed a "second bill of rights" — right to employment, fair wages, decent housing, medical care, and education. Never enacted but influenced postwar liberal politics and the GI Bill. Represented FDR's vision of social democracy for postwar America.
tap to expand
1944
GI Bill of Rights
Servicemen's Readjustment Act (1944) — provided veterans with college tuition, low-interest home loans, and unemployment benefits. Created the postwar middle class; 8 million veterans attended college. However benefits were administered locally and often denied to Black veterans — deepened racial wealth gap.
tap to expand
1942
Bracero Program
1942–1964 guest worker program importing Mexican agricultural laborers to replace drafted American workers. 4.6 million contracts; workers often exploited. Demonstrates wartime labor contradictions — needed Mexican labor but simultaneously deported "illegal aliens." Laid groundwork for postwar immigration patterns.
tap to expand
1943
Zoot Suit Riots
June 1943 LA — white sailors attacked Mexican-American youth wearing zoot suits, which they deemed unpatriotic (excess fabric). Police arrested victims, not attackers. Symbolized racial violence against Latinos, wartime racial tensions, and the limits of the "Good War" ideology of equality.
tap to expand
1942–1945
Navajo Code Talkers
Native Americans (primarily Navajo) recruited to develop and transmit military communications in their native language — unbreakable code. Contributed to victories at Iwo Jima and elsewhere. Irony: same people whose language and culture had been suppressed in federal boarding schools were now using it to win the war.
tap to expand
1942
Executive Order 9066
FDR's order authorizing the internment of 120,000 Japanese Americans on the West Coast — 70,000 were U.S. citizens. Forced into camps; lost homes, businesses, and property. No charges, no trials. One of the worst civil liberties violations in American history; Congress apologized in 1988 (Civil Liberties Act).
tap to expand
1944
Korematsu v. United States
SCOTUS upheld EO 9066 in a 6-3 decision (1944), ruling national security interests outweighed individual rights. Dissents by Murphy and Jackson were powerful. Formally overruled by Trump v. Hawaii (2018). Represents SCOTUS's failure to protect civil liberties during wartime and racial hysteria.
tap to expand
1942–1945
Double-V Campaign
Pittsburgh Courier's 1942 campaign: "Double Victory" — victory abroad over fascism AND victory at home over racism. Linked WWII to civil rights struggle; argued that fighting fascism abroad while tolerating Jim Crow at home was hypocritical. Set agenda for postwar civil rights movement.
tap to expand
1942–1945
Manhattan Project
Top-secret program to develop atomic weapons — 130,000 workers, $2B, sites in TN, WA, NM (Los Alamos). Led by Oppenheimer; Einstein's letter prompted it. Produced the bombs dropped on Hiroshima (Aug. 6) and Nagasaki (Aug. 9), 1945. Inaugurated nuclear age and Cold War arms race.
tap to expand
1945
Yalta Conference
Feb. 1945 — FDR, Churchill, Stalin. Agreed on post-war occupation zones in Germany, free elections in Eastern Europe (Stalin ignored this), Soviet entry against Japan, and United Nations. Later seen as a "sell-out" to Stalin; set stage for Cold War divisions. FDR died 2 months later.
tap to expand
1944
Bretton Woods Conference
July 1944 — 44 nations. Created IMF (International Monetary Fund) and World Bank; established dollar as global reserve currency (tied to gold). Designed postwar international economic order to prevent another Great Depression. Cemented U.S. economic dominance in postwar world until Nixon ended gold standard (1971).
tap to expand

Section II

Learning Objectives & Broader Context

The College Board's framework for Period 7, organized chronologically with context and key events.

7.1 · 1890s

Contextualizing Period 7

Explain the context in which America grew into its role as a world power.

By 1890, the American frontier was declared closed (Frederick Jackson Turner). Industrialization had created enormous wealth and corporate power. European imperial powers were carving up Africa and Asia. Alfred Thayer Mahan's The Influence of Sea Power (1890) argued for naval expansion and overseas bases. The U.S. was poised to enter global competition for empire.

7.2 · 1890s–1900s

Imperialism: Debates

Explain the similarities and differences in attitudes about the nation's proper role in the world.

Imperialists (TR, Lodge, Mahan): racial Anglo-Saxon superiority, commercial markets, naval power, "civilizing mission." Anti-Imperialists (Twain, Carnegie, Jane Addams): contradicted democratic values, exploited non-white peoples, threatened the republic. The debate over annexing the Philippines (1898–1902) crystallized these arguments.

7.3 · 1898

The Spanish–American War

Explain the effects of the Spanish–American War.

Triggered by yellow journalism (Hearst, Pulitzer), the sinking of the USS Maine, and Cuban independence. "Splendid little war" — U.S. acquired Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines. TR's Rough Riders at San Juan Hill. Philippine-American War (1899–1902) followed — brutal counterinsurgency. U.S. became a formal Pacific and Caribbean empire.

7.4 · 1900–1920

The Progressives

Compare the goals and effects of the Progressive reform movement. Compare attitudes toward natural resource use from 1890 to 1945.

Urban, middle-class reform movement addressing industrial capitalism's excesses. Key reforms: direct democracy (initiative, referendum, recall), direct election of senators (17th Amendment), women's suffrage (19th Amendment), income tax (16th Amendment), antitrust, food safety, labor protections, and conservation. TR = "New Nationalism" (federal regulation); Wilson = "New Freedom" (restore competition); Taft more conservative.

7.5 · 1917–1918

World War I: Military and Diplomacy

Explain the causes and consequences of U.S. involvement in World War I.

U.S. entered April 1917: unrestricted submarine warfare + Zimmermann Telegram. 2 million U.S. troops (AEF) under Pershing. Broke stalemate; Germany collapsed Nov. 1918. Paris Peace Conference — Wilson's 14 Points vs. Clemenceau's punitive demands. Treaty of Versailles: war guilt, reparations, territorial losses. Senate rejected League; U.S. returned to "normalcy."

7.6 · 1917–1920

World War I: Home Front

Explain the causes and effects of international and internal migration patterns over time.

Great Migration: 1.6 million Black Americans moved north (1910–1930) for factory jobs and escape from Jim Crow — transformed cities like Chicago, New York, Detroit. War industries brought women into workforce. CPI propaganda stoked xenophobia. Espionage/Sedition Acts suppressed dissent. 1919: race riots (Chicago, Tulsa), Red Scare, steel strikes — "watershed year."

7.7 · 1920s

1920s: Innovations in Communication and Technology

Explain the causes and effects of innovations in communication and technology in the United States over time.

Mass production (Ford's Model T, assembly line) made consumer goods affordable. Radio (first commercial station KDKA, 1920) created national mass culture. Hollywood and talkies. Advertising industry. Consumer credit / installment buying. These created the "new society" of the 1920s — but also overproduction and debt that contributed to the Depression crash.

7.8 · 1920s

1920s: Cultural and Political Controversies

Explain the causes and effects of international and internal migration patterns over time.

Cultural fault lines: fundamentalism vs. modernism (Scopes Trial, 1925); nativism vs. pluralism (KKK revival, 1924 Immigration Act); tradition vs. modernity (Prohibition, flappers, Harlem Renaissance). "100 percent Americanism" vs. New Negro. The prosperity decade concealed deep social fractures that the Depression would tear open.

7.9 · 1929–1933

The Great Depression

Explain the causes of the Great Depression and its effects on the economy.

Causes: stock speculation, overproduction, agricultural crisis, weak banking system, overextended consumer credit, Smoot-Hawley tariff (1930), global economic interconnection. Stock market crash Oct. 1929. By 1933: 25% unemployment, 5,000 bank failures, GDP fell 30%. Hoover's response inadequate — Reconstruction Finance Corp, but opposed direct relief. Bonus March (1932) destroyed Hoover.

7.10 · 1933–1939

The New Deal

Explain how the Great Depression and the New Deal impacted American political, social, and economic life over time.

FDR's New Deal fundamentally transformed the federal government's role. First New Deal (1933–34): emergency relief and economic recovery. Second New Deal (1935–36): structural reforms (Social Security, Wagner Act, WPA) — more redistributive, responding to critics from left (Huey Long) and right. New Deal coalition: labor, urban immigrants, South, African Americans. Legacy: modern regulatory/welfare state.

7.11 · 1919–1941

Interwar Foreign Policy

Explain the similarities and differences in attitudes about the nation's proper role in the world.

U.S. retreated into isolationism: rejected League, Washington Naval Treaty (1922), Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928, outlawed war). Neutrality Acts (1935–37) tried to prevent another WWI entanglement. FDR's Good Neighbor Policy improved Latin American relations. As Hitler rose, isolationists (America First, Lindbergh) vs. interventionists. Lend-Lease (1941) and Atlantic Charter moved U.S. toward Allies before Pearl Harbor.

7.12 · 1941–1945

World War II: Mobilization

Explain how and why U.S. participation in WWII transformed American society.

Pearl Harbor (Dec. 7, 1941) unified the nation. War production ended the Depression — GDP doubled, unemployment to 1%. Women (Rosie the Riveter) and minorities entered industrial workforce. Internment of Japanese Americans (EO 9066). Double-V campaign. Bracero program. GI Bill transformed postwar society. Military spending created the military-industrial complex Eisenhower would warn about in 1961.

7.13 · 1942–1945

World War II: Military

Explain the causes and effects of the victory of the United States and its allies over the Axis powers.

Two-front war: Europe (North Africa → Italy → D-Day → Berlin) and Pacific (island hopping: Guadalcanal → Iwo Jima → Okinawa). Eastern Front: USSR bore the brunt (27M dead). Allied unity despite tensions (second front debate). Manhattan Project → Hiroshima (Aug. 6) and Nagasaki (Aug. 9) → Japanese surrender Sept. 2, 1945. V-E Day May 8, 1945.

7.14 · 1944–1945

Postwar Diplomacy

Explain the consequences of U.S. involvement in World War II.

Bretton Woods (1944) — dollar-centered international monetary system; World Bank and IMF. Yalta (Feb. 1945) — Allied occupation zones, UN, Soviet promise of free elections in Eastern Europe (broken). UN founded (April 1945). Nuremberg Trials established individual accountability for war crimes. U.S. emerged as dominant global superpower; tensions with USSR immediately evident.

7.15 · Synthesis

Comparison in Period 7

Compare the relative significance of the major events of the first half of the 20th century in shaping American identity.

Key comparison themes: continuity and change in federal power (Progressive Era → New Deal), debates over American internationalism (imperialism → isolationism → WWII), struggles for civil rights and equality (NAACP → Double-V → postwar movement), and the tension between American ideals and American practice (Four Freedoms vs. Jim Crow, internment, exclusion).

DBQ — Document-Based Question

Documents, sample thesis/body paragraphs, and a graphic organizer for Period 7.

What the DBQ tests: Your ability to construct a historically defensible argument using at least 3 of the provided documents + outside evidence. You must: (1) write a thesis with a line of reasoning, (2) contextualize, (3) use evidence from docs, (4) use evidence beyond the docs (HAPP), (5) demonstrate complex understanding (comparison, continuity/change, cause/effect, or historiography).

Scoring: 1 pt thesis · 1 pt contextualization · 2 pts evidence (docs) · 1 pt evidence (beyond) · 1 pt HAPP (Historical Argument Perspective/Purpose) · 1 pt complexity = 7 points total

📄 Primary Source Documents

Doc 1 Alfred Thayer Mahan — The Influence of Sea Power upon History 1890

Source: Alfred Thayer Mahan, naval officer and strategist, 1890

"Whether they will or not, Americans must now begin to look outward. The growing production of the country demands it. An increase of commerce will follow the line of greatest yield. Such increase must be supported by a merchant marine, which again will be backed by naval power."
HAPP Analysis: Mahan was a naval officer writing for military and political audiences. His purpose was to advocate for U.S. imperial expansion and naval buildup. His argument was deeply influential on TR, Lodge, and the imperialist movement. Use this doc to argue for commercial/strategic motivations for American imperialism.
Doc 2 Platform of the Anti-Imperialist League 1899

Source: Anti-Imperialist League, October 1899

"We hold that the policy known as imperialism is hostile to liberty and tends toward militarism, an evil from which it has been our glory to be free. We regret that it has become necessary in the land of Washington and Lincoln to reaffirm that all men, of whatever race or color, are entitled to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness."
HAPP Analysis: Written by prominent Americans including Andrew Carnegie and Mark Twain. Purpose: oppose annexation of Philippines. Invokes founding ideals against imperialism. Use to argue that expansion contradicted American democratic values and created a hypocritical foreign policy.
Doc 3 Woodrow Wilson — War Message to Congress 1917

Source: Woodrow Wilson, Address to Congress, April 2, 1917

"The world must be made safe for democracy. Its peace must be planted upon the tested foundations of political liberty. We have no selfish ends to serve. We desire no conquest, no dominion. We seek no indemnities for ourselves, no material compensation for the sacrifices we shall freely make."
HAPP Analysis: Wilson framing U.S. entry into WWI in idealistic, democratic terms for Congress and the public. Purpose: build political and popular support for war. Contrast this idealism with the Espionage/Sedition Acts passed weeks later. Use to discuss the tension between war aims and civil liberties.
Doc 4 Franklin D. Roosevelt — First Inaugural Address 1933

Source: Franklin D. Roosevelt, First Inaugural Address, March 4, 1933

"The only thing we have to fear is fear itself — nameless, unreasoning, unjustified terror which paralyzes needed efforts to convert retreat into advance... Our greatest primary task is to put people to work."
HAPP Analysis: FDR as new president addressing a nation in crisis (25% unemployment, bank failures). Purpose: restore confidence and signal activist government. Use to analyze the New Deal's transformation of government's role and the political context of the Great Depression.
Doc 5 FDR — Four Freedoms Speech (State of the Union) 1941

Source: Franklin D. Roosevelt, Annual Message to Congress, January 6, 1941

"In the future days, which we seek to make secure, we look forward to a world founded upon four essential human freedoms... freedom of speech and expression... freedom of every person to worship God in his own way... freedom from want... freedom from fear."
HAPP Analysis: FDR justifying Lend-Lease and preparing public opinion for possible U.S. entry into WWII. Audience: Congress and the American public. Use this to discuss the ideological stakes of WWII and the irony of fighting for freedoms denied to Black Americans, Japanese Americans, and others at home.
Doc 6 Pittsburgh Courier — Double-V Campaign Editorial 1942

Source: James G. Thompson, letter published in the Pittsburgh Courier, January 31, 1942

"Should I sacrifice my life to live half American? The V for Victory sign is being displayed prominently in all so-called democratic countries... let colored Americans adopt the double VV for a double victory. The first V for victory over our enemies from without, the second V for victory over our enemies from within."
HAPP Analysis: Black American civilian writing to a Black newspaper weeks after Pearl Harbor. Purpose: challenge the contradiction of fighting for democracy abroad while denied it at home. Use to argue that WWII both exposed American racial hypocrisy and accelerated the civil rights movement.
Doc 7 Justice Robert Jackson — Korematsu v. U.S. (Dissent) 1944

Source: Justice Robert H. Jackson, dissent in Korematsu v. United States, 1944

"A military order, however unconstitutional, is not apt to last longer than the military emergency... But once a judicial opinion rationalizes such an order to show that it conforms to the Constitution, or rather rationalizes the Constitution to show that the Constitution sanctions such an order, the court for all time has validated the principle."
HAPP Analysis: A Supreme Court justice dissenting from the majority opinion upholding Japanese American internment. Purpose: warning about judicial precedent and constitutional erosion. Use to discuss the limits of constitutional protection during wartime and the broader civil liberties failures of WWII.

✍ Sample DBQ Responses

Before you read: A strong DBQ thesis (1) makes a historically defensible claim, (2) establishes a clear line of reasoning, and (3) does NOT simply restate the prompt. Your contextualization paragraph should come before your thesis and discuss broader historical context from before the documents' timeframe.
Sample A Thesis: American Foreign Policy & Empire, 1890–1945 Full Credit Thesis
Thesis From 1890 to 1945, American foreign policy was driven primarily by economic interests and strategic competition with European powers, but was consistently framed in the language of democratic idealism — a contradiction that shaped both global outcomes and domestic civil liberties, as seen in the tension between Wilson's Fourteen Points and the Espionage Act, and between FDR's Four Freedoms and the internment of Japanese Americans.

Why this works: It makes a specific, defensible argument (economic interests + strategic competition framed as democratic idealism), establishes a clear line of reasoning (the contradiction between ideals and practice), and previews evidence it will use (Wilson, Espionage Act, Four Freedoms, internment). It does not simply describe — it argues.

Body paragraph tip: Each body paragraph should open with a topic sentence that supports your thesis. Use at least one document + HAPP analysis per paragraph. Then bring in outside evidence (something not in the documents). Aim for 3 body paragraphs with 2–3 docs used per paragraph.
Sample B Contextualization Paragraph: New Deal & Government's Role Contextualization Example
Contextualization Paragraph The Great Depression did not emerge in a vacuum. Throughout the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the Progressive movement had gradually expanded the federal government's role in regulating the economy and protecting workers — from TR's antitrust suits and the Pure Food and Drug Act (1906) to Wilson's Federal Reserve Act (1913) and Clayton Antitrust Act (1914). However, the 1920s represented a sharp conservative reaction: under Harding and Coolidge, government systematically retreated from regulation, embraced "welfare capitalism" and the open shop, and cut taxes on the wealthy. This decade of unfettered corporate power — combined with agricultural crisis, consumer debt, and stock speculation — created the structural conditions for catastrophic economic collapse. When the Depression hit in 1929, the insufficiency of Hoover's response (limited to the Reconstruction Finance Corporation, refusing direct relief) demonstrated that the 1920s model of minimal government had fundamentally failed, setting the stage for FDR's radical expansion of federal authority.
Contextualization rules: Must describe broader historical context before or around the time frame of the prompt. Must be a full, developed paragraph — not just a sentence. Must be meaningfully connected to the argument, not just background noise.
Sample C Body Paragraph: Civil Liberties & WWII (Using Doc 6 + Outside Evidence) Evidence + HAPP + Outside Evidence
Body Paragraph The tension between America's democratic war aims and its domestic racial oppression was perhaps the defining contradiction of WWII. As James G. Thompson wrote in the Pittsburgh Courier in January 1942, Black Americans demanded a "Double-V" — victory abroad against fascism and victory at home against racism. Thompson, writing as a Black civilian to a Black newspaper audience just weeks after Pearl Harbor, was explicitly challenging the government's claim that the war was a struggle for universal freedom. The irony he identified was structural: the same government that broadcast the Four Freedoms (Document 5) was enforcing Jim Crow in the military, excluding Black workers from defense industry jobs until A. Philip Randolph threatened a March on Washington in 1941 (outside evidence — Executive Order 8802), and within months would intern 120,000 Japanese Americans under Executive Order 9066 with no charges or trial (Document 7, Jackson dissent). This contradiction did not resolve itself during the war — but it planted the seeds of the postwar civil rights movement, as returning Black veterans who had fought fascism abroad refused to accept second-class citizenship at home.
Scoring check: ✓ Uses Doc 6 with HAPP (who wrote it, for whom, why) · ✓ References Doc 5 and Doc 7 · ✓ Brings in outside evidence (EO 8802, March on Washington threat) · ✓ Connects to thesis (contradiction between democratic ideals and practice) · ✓ Addresses complexity (seeds of civil rights movement)

Complexity Point Strategies

Continuity & Change: How did the federal government's role in the economy change from 1890 to 1945?
Comparison: Compare Progressive Era reforms to New Deal reforms — similarities, differences, what changed?
Causation: How did WWI cause the conditions that led to the Great Depression?
Corroboration: Use multiple docs that point to the same argument from different perspectives.
Qualification: Acknowledge a counterargument — e.g., "While the New Deal transformed federal power, it consistently excluded Black Americans from full benefits."

Section IV

DBQ Do's & Don'ts

Based on the College Board's updated 2024 scoring guidelines — what earns points and what loses them.

✓ Do These Things
✓
ThesisWrite a thesis that makes a historically defensible claim AND establishes a clear line of reasoning. It can be more than one sentence. Place it in your intro OR conclusion.
✓
ThesisMention at least two different interpretations of the question and offer a judgment about which part of your argument is most significant.
✓
ContextualizationWrite a full, developed paragraph connecting broader historical context to your argument — from before or around the time period of the prompt. Must be meaningfully connected, not just background.
✓
EvidenceUse content from at least 3 documents to address the topic (1 pt). Use at least 4 documents to support your argument with explanation (2 pts). Show how each doc supports your specific claim.
✓
Outside EvidenceInclude at least one piece of evidence not in the documents that supports your argument. This must be a specific historical fact, not a vague reference.
✓
HAPP (APCP)For at least 2 documents, explain how or why the document's Historical Situation, Audience, Purpose, or Point of View is relevant to your argument — not just "this person believed X."
✓
ComplexityChoose ONE pathway: (a) demonstrate nuance — highlight contradictions, connect across periods, explain both cause & effect; (b) explain HAPP for 4 documents; or (c) use all 7 documents as evidence.
✓
StructureOrganize around your line of reasoning, not around each document in order. Group documents thematically to support your argument.
✗ Avoid These Mistakes
✗
ThesisDon't restate the prompt as your thesis. "This essay will discuss the causes of WWI" earns zero points. You must take a position and establish a line of reasoning.
✗
ThesisDon't write a thesis that can't be argued — "The New Deal had many effects" is not defensible. A thesis must be specific enough that someone could disagree with it.
✗
ContextualizationDon't write only one sentence of context. The College Board requires a full, developed explanation — a single sentence will not earn the point.
✗
EvidenceDon't just describe or summarize documents. Graders will not award evidence points for students who walk through each document in order without connecting to an argument.
✗
EvidenceDon't misattribute or misread documents. Misidentifying the author or misrepresenting a document's content can undermine your entire argument and lose points.
✗
HAPPDon't just name the author's identity. "This was written by a politician" earns nothing. Explain WHY their position, purpose, or situation makes the document say what it says.
✗
ComplexityDon't tack on a complexity sentence at the end. Complexity must be woven into your argument throughout — a disconnected final paragraph will not earn the point.
✗
StructureDon't write 7 body paragraphs, one per document. This almost always results in summary, not argument. Aim for 3 thematic body paragraphs with multiple documents per paragraph.

2024 Rubric Update — Key Changes

The College Board updated the APUSH DBQ rubric for 2023–24. You now need 4 documents (down from 6) for full evidence points, and 2 HAPP analyses (down from 3). The complexity point now has three alternative pathways: demonstrate nuance, explain HAPP for 4 docs, or cite all 7 documents. The DBQ is worth 25% of your total exam score.
